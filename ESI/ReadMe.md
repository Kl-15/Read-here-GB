# NABARD Grade A

This document consolidates the attached guidebook with current web references to create a practical reference for the NABARD Grade A Generalist exam, covering pay, pattern, syllabus, priority topics, and job profile.

## Exam structure

NABARD Grade A is a three-stage process: Phase 1, Phase 2, and Interview, with a psychometric test before the interview. Phase 1 is only for shortlisting, while final merit is based on Phase 2 plus Interview.

### Phase 1: merit vs non-merit

Phase 1 has 200 questions for 200 marks with 120 minutes and 0.25 negative marking for each wrong answer. The paper is divided into **non-merit/qualifying** and **merit** sections.

| Category | Subjects | Questions | Marks | Role in selection |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| Non-merit / qualifying | Reasoning, English, Computer Knowledge, Quantitative Aptitude, Decision Making | 100 | 100 | Must clear sectional cut-offs; not counted in final merit. |
| Merit | General Awareness, Economic & Social Issues, Agriculture & Rural Development | 100 | 100 | Used to determine overall Phase 1 shortlisting cut-off. |

### Phase 2

Phase 2 has two papers carrying a total of 200 marks, and these marks count toward final selection. Paper 1 is Descriptive English for 100 marks in 90 minutes, while Paper 2 combines ESI and ARD with objective and descriptive components for 100 marks in 120 minutes.

### Interview

Candidates who clear Phase 2 take a psychometric test and then an in-person interview carrying 50 marks. Final selection is based on Phase 2 plus Interview, making descriptive performance and interview quality decisive.

## Job profile and work life

According to the guidebook, NABARD Grade A officers work across rural development, agricultural credit, grant and loan scrutiny, proposal evaluation, due diligence, institutional coordination, credit planning, monitoring, and district-level credit deployment. Department allocation depends on NABARD's requirements, so the exact role varies across assignments.

### Core responsibilities

- Preliminary scrutiny of loan and grant applications.
- Evaluation of proposals from agencies seeking NABARD support and presentation to senior management.
- Coordination with banks, NGOs, deputy commissioners, municipalities, and state bodies.
- Planning, monitoring, and coordination of credit-related activities.
- Supporting district-level credit flow and deployment strategies.

### Training, posting, transfers, promotion

Selected officers undergo a 2-week induction at the National Bank Staff College inside BIRD, Lucknow, with salary payable from day one. Postings are typically at the Mumbai head office or around 30 regional offices, mostly in urban or state-capital locations, while transfers usually occur every 3–5 years depending on operational needs. The guidebook also highlights a structured promotion path, though actual progression depends on vacancy availability.

### Perks and benefits

The guidebook lists concessional loans, medical reimbursement, higher-studies reimbursement after probation if aligned with NABARD's goals, group term insurance, accumulated leave benefits, and possible study leave subject to conditions. These benefits materially improve the practical attractiveness of the role beyond base salary alone.

## Syllabus: full view

The syllabus below combines the guidebook's detailed topic list with the merit/non-merit logic relevant for preparation.

### Phase 1 non-merit / qualifying syllabus

These sections require sectional cut-off clearance, but their marks are not counted in the final merit list.

#### Reasoning

Topics include alphanumeric series, coding-decoding, syllogism, direction sense, inequality, ranking and order, blood relation, puzzles, seating arrangement, input-output, critical reasoning, data sufficiency, and statement-based questions.

#### Quantitative Aptitude

Topics include ratio and proportion, percentage, profit and loss, simple and compound interest, average and ages, time and work, pipes and cistern, time and distance, mixture and alligation, series and quadratic equation, mathematical inequalities, number series, data interpretation, simplification/approximation, and quantity comparisons.

#### English

Topics include reading comprehension, sentence rearrangement, cloze test, para jumbles, double fillers, error detection, sentence improvement, and vocabulary such as synonyms, antonyms, one-word substitution, and phrasal verbs.

#### Computer Knowledge

Topics include computer awareness, hardware and memory, software and operating systems, MS Office, DBMS, internet and computer security, computer history and generations, and shortcuts.

#### Decision Making

Topics include basics of decision making, types of decision making, decision-making models, approaches, factors affecting decision making, and types of problems in decision making.

### Phase 1 merit syllabus

These sections decide Phase 1 shortlisting and therefore deserve the highest preparation priority.

#### General Awareness

Coverage includes current affairs of the preceding six months, national and international news, defence, important days, appointments and obituaries, sports, science and technology, awards, environment, banking, economy, schemes, books and authors, plus static GA like capitals, currencies, headquarters, national parks, taglines, and power plants.

#### Economic and Social Issues (ESI)

ESI covers nature of the Indian economy; underdevelopment; globalisation; reforms; privatisation; inflation and inflation trends; poverty alleviation and employment generation; population trends and population policy; agriculture and food security; industry and labour policy; rural banking and financial institutions; reforms in banking/financial sector; IMF, World Bank, WTO and regional cooperation; social structure; migration; gender issues; education; health; environment; social justice; positive discrimination; social movements; Indian political systems; human development; and current economic and social issues.

#### Agriculture and Rural Development (ARD)

ARD covers agriculture and agronomy, cropping systems, dryland agriculture, seed production and seed village, agrometeorology, precision farming, SRI, organic farming, soil and water conservation, watershed management, irrigation types and techniques, farm engineering, storage and processing, horticulture, animal husbandry, fisheries, forestry, agricultural extension, climate change, the present scenario of Indian agriculture and allied activities, and rural development including Panchayati Raj, MGNREGA, NRLM, drinking water, sanitation, housing, PURA, and related programmes.

### Phase 2 syllabus

#### Paper 1: Descriptive English

The descriptive English syllabus includes essay, précis writing, comprehension, and business/office correspondence.

#### Paper 2: ESI + ARD

Paper 2 uses the same broad ESI and ARD syllabus but tests it through both objective and descriptive questions, making concept application and current-affairs linkage more important than bare memorization.

## Priority-wise topic classification

The guidebook gives a highly usable topic-priority map, especially for ESI and ARD. The classification below is adapted into a preparation-first structure.

## ESI priority classification

### Must master

- Government schemes related to poverty, employment, social security, health, education, and financial inclusion, because current-scheme questions dominate ESI.
- ESI current affairs, including inflation trends, GDP and growth projections, unemployment and poverty data, economic reports, and social-sector indicators.
- Monetary policy, fiscal policy, inflation, CPI vs WPI, poverty, unemployment, national income concepts, budget basics, and basic financial institutions such as RBI, IMF, World Bank, and ADB.

### Frequently asked

- Human development, social justice, demographic trends, urbanisation and migration, and education-health-environment linkages from the static syllabus.
- Banking and financial-sector reforms, rural banking, and financial inclusion themes.

### Nice to know

- Broader globalization architecture, regional economic cooperation, and deeper industry/public sector theory that appears less frequently in direct question form.

### Safe to deprioritize in a short window

- Deep theoretical economics beyond the basic syllabus-linked concepts, especially if it does not connect to recent schemes, reports, or data.

## ARD priority classification

### Must master

The guidebook explicitly identifies these as the **core 8–10 chapters** and says they deserve the highest priority because roughly 20–25 questions come from them.

- Agronomy and field crops.
- Farming systems.
- Soil and water conservation.
- Plantation and horticulture crops.
- Animal husbandry and poultry.
- Fisheries.
- Forestry.
- Agriculture extension.
- Irrigation.
- Seeds and sowing.

### Frequently asked

- ARD current affairs, which the guidebook treats as non-negotiable and highly relevant in both Phase 1 and Phase 2.
- Agricultural finance and marketing, food security, farm management, and rural development programmes such as MGNREGA, NRLM, rural housing, drinking water, and Swachh Bharat.
- Climate change, ecology, carbon credits, NAPCC, SAPCC, UNFCCC, and agriculture-livelihood impact.

### Nice to know

- Peripheral chapters such as environment and ecology basics, basic genetics/plant-breeding-type facts, animal diseases, dairy and milking basics, and selected agricultural engineering numericals.

### Safe to deprioritize in a short window

- Highly technical subtopics that are neither core static chapters nor recurring current-affairs areas, unless they repeatedly appear in mocks or PYQs.

## Merit vs non-merit preparation logic

The guidebook is clear that Phase 1 merit comes only from GA, ESI, and ARD, while non-merit sections only require sectional clearance. For short-window preparation, the highest ROI lies in maximizing ESI, ARD, schemes, reports, and current affairs, then covering finance-linked GA, and only afterward bringing non-merit sections safely above cut-off.

### Practical study-order from the guidebook

| Priority | Area | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | ARD Static core chapters | Biggest static return; guidebook says 20–25 questions can come from these areas. |
| 2 | ARD Current Affairs | Non-negotiable in both phases; major source of marks. |
| 3 | ESI Current Affairs + Schemes | Majority of ESI questions come from current affairs and schemes. |
| 4 | ESI Static basics | Needed for concept support in inflation, poverty, national income, policy, and budget. |
| 5 | GA + Finance | Important, but relatively more random; aim for controlled marks rather than perfection. |
| 6 | Non-merit sections | Only need safe sectional clearance, not over-preparation. |
| 7 | Descriptive answer writing | Critical because Phase 2 and final merit depend heavily on descriptive scores. |

## Competition and cut-off context

The guidebook shows that the number of general-stream vacancies has fluctuated, with 80 vacancies in 2022–23, 77 in 2023–24, 50 in 2024–25, and 40 in 2025–26. It also notes that a significant share of applicants do not appear for Phase 1, and another share of qualified candidates skip Phase 2, which lowers effective competition compared with raw application counts.

Final cut-offs for the General category moved from 164.33 in 2021 to 171.50 in 2022, 172.75 in 2023, and 168.00 in 2024, indicating that Phase 2 plus Interview performance remains the real differentiator.

## Eligibility snapshot

For the General stream, the guidebook states the age limit is 21 to 30 years for the unreserved category, with relaxation for reserved categories as per notification. Educational eligibility broadly includes a bachelor's degree with 60 percent marks, or postgraduate/MBA/PGDM with 55 percent marks, or CA/CS/ICWA/PhD from recognized institutions, subject to category relaxations. Only Indian citizens can apply, and the notification does not specify a maximum number of attempts as long as eligibility conditions are met.
